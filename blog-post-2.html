Are contactless menus the future?

The COVID-19 pandemic has changed many of the ways we do business. Health issues among customers and business owners have encouraged the adoption of social distancing and other methods to prevent physical contact. These changes have been particularly difficult for the hotel and catering industry, where physical proximity is the norm. That is why I think contactless menus are the future!


How contactless menus work?


1. A customer enters the restaurant and is shown by the host.
2. The host points to the QR code on the table and gives a brief explanation.
3. Customers scan QR codes.
4. The customer places their order through the QR Zebre Pro menu application.
5. The customer uses the menu app to pay for the food.

"People have a lot of hygiene and health care. It forced the industry to change, especially restaurants. They had to turn to technology to find solutions."



Enter a contactless menu - a digital version of a traditional physical menu that can be accessed via a link or QR code. Some contactless menus go beyond a straight food menu, offering a great way to ask the wait staff, order food and drinks, and even pay for the food. These digital menus and ordering tools have become a valuable asset to restaurants, and their use has increased significantly. What are the benefits of contactless menus?


Implementing a contactless menu has many benefits for the catering industry:



Quick Service: The contactless menu allows customers to order as soon as they are ready instead of waiting for the staff to come to the table. Efficient use of staff time: Since customers rely on contactless menus, servers can cover several tables at the same time.

Improved Customer Experience: Between faster service and more table coverage, there is less waiting for customers. "This is especially useful during peak times like Friday evenings," Kouumdjiev says. "Customers hate waiting, but a contactless menu can help everyone avoid disappointment."

Increased revenue: Kouumdjiev said sample sizes are getting bigger at restaurants using contactless menus for two reasons. For one thing, there is less friction in the ordering experience - consider that you can click the order button again instead of waiting for the server to be available. Second, upsell options like extra cheese or bacon are easy to promote on screen instead of relying on the server to announce them.


What types of businesses use contactless menus?


Restaurants are the first to use contactless menus. But various restaurants - such as fast food places and cafes - have extended this concept. "Any place with a restaurant where people want to prevent physical contact can use a no-contact menu. It is this broad application that led me to find an unlikely market for contactless menus: the hotel industry.

Hotel restaurants have clearly benefited from these menus, but when they implemented a contactless menu in one of these companies, he faced an interesting question that prompted business expansion. A hotel worker casually says, "I wish you could put a QR code in the room." Then I immediately realized that the indoor service offered a perfect opportunity for a contactless menu, so I told my team that I could do that. Even if physical interaction is not usually a concern, these menus can still increase performance.

How do contactless menus work?
Most contactless menus work the same way. Here is an example view using a restaurant (assuming that the menu item includes the order). Just open the camera on your phone and scan the QR code below!


A guest enters a restaurant and the host shows him his table. The host points to the QR code on the table and briefly explains how to use the smartphone to access the restaurant's menu.The customer scans the QR code and browses the contactless menu on their phone. The visitor sets their order through the menu application or with the supervisor.

The customer uses the menu app to pay for their meal immediately after the meal (or any time after it is served). One of the advantages of the advanced contactless menu is that customers can pay for their orders, whether they order through the app or on the server. "In addition, these menu items allow customers to split their bill, freeing up servers to spend time elsewhere."
